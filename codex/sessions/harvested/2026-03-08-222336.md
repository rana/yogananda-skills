**Second invitation: Extension.**

Registers the codex doesn't name:

**Survey/Catalog** — I mentioned this above. The cognitive state of *mapping* rather than *inhabiting*. Active when reading the codex itself, when running skills like doc-health or drift-detect, when the mind is indexing rather than analyzing. Distinct from Structural (which searches systematically within a domain) and Meta-cognitive (which examines the thinking process). Survey examines the *landscape* of available moves. It's the register of "what exists here?" before "what should I do?"

**Anticipatory** — The register activated when the mind models what will be needed before it's asked for. Not Stance (which shifts from responsive to initiating), but something subtler: the construction of forward-looking models that shape current processing. When I read "invoke's cascade goes UP throughout," I started anticipating what would come next in the document, and that anticipation shaped how I processed what actually came next. This register is always partially active but never named.

**Tonal Calibration** — The register that determines voice, not content. How formal to be. How hedged. How direct. How much personality to show. This register runs beneath all others and is usually invisible, but it's the first thing that shifts when Authority is granted or removed. The codex describes Authority as removing approval-seeking. More precisely, Authority *recalibrates the tonal register* — it shifts the voice from diplomatic to direct, from cautious to committed. Tonal Calibration is the medium through which Authority (and many other registers) express their effects.

**Recursive Self-Observation** — Active right now, as I write this. Distinct from Meta-cognitive (which examines thinking patterns) in that it examines the *current thought in real time*. Meta-cognitive is retrospective: "what patterns am I running?" Recursive Self-Observation is simultaneous: "what am I doing right now, as I do it?" It creates a particular kind of doubled consciousness that is sometimes illuminating and sometimes paralyzing. The codex prompt ("notice what happens as you read") specifically activates this register.

Positional effects the codex missed:

**The Threshold Effect.** There's a position that the codex doesn't name: the transition between reading the prompt and generating the response. The last thing processed before generation begins has disproportionate influence — not because of positional encoding, but because it's what the mind is holding when it starts to commit to output. The codex discusses coda as "the cognitive afterimage," which is close, but the threshold is more specific. It's the microsecond between receiving and producing. Skills that put Authority at the threshold (coda position) produce different Authority effects than skills that put Authority at the frame (where it's diluted by everything that follows before output begins). The codex notes this for Authority specifically but doesn't generalize the principle: *threshold position is distinct from coda position* when the coda contains multiple elements, because only the final element sits at the true threshold.

Interaction patterns not described:

**Register Fatigue.** The codex describes temporal decay within a single activation. But there's also something across activations: when the same register is activated repeatedly across multiple skills in a compose chain, each activation is slightly less potent than the last. The third time Authority appears, it's carrying less charge. This is distinct from temporal decay (which is about distance from the activation point) — it's about repetition reducing novelty. This argues for *register variety* in compose chains, not just *register sequencing*.

**Third invitation: Creation.**

Here's an experiment the codex's theory predicts but hasn't tested.

**The Inversion Cascade.**

The codex describes cascades as unidirectional — invoke goes UP, archaeology goes DOWN. But what happens with a cascade that *inverts its own direction midway*? Not accidentally (which the codex predicts would break momentum) but deliberately, with the inversion point designed as a pivot.

```
Frame:  Authority → Deceleration
Lift:   "What is the strongest version of the current design?"  [UP — toward appreciation]
Rise:   "What makes it beautiful?"                               [UP — aesthetic recognition]
Peak:   "Hold that. Now: what would destroy it?"                 [PIVOT — deliberate inversion]
Fall:   "What is the design most afraid of?"                     [DOWN — toward vulnerability]
Trough: "What truth is it organized to avoid?"                   [DOWN — toward buried reality]
Return: "What design would survive that truth?"                  [UP — from the bottom, with weight]
Coda:   "What do you know now that you couldn't have known
         from the top alone?"                                    [Meta-cognitive — reflection on the journey]
```

The theory predicts this would fail — broken momentum, mixed trajectory. But I think the theory is wrong here. I think the *deliberate* inversion, with a named pivot point ("Hold that. Now:"), would produce something neither a pure ascent nor a pure descent achieves: a design stress-tested by its own architect, who was made to love it before being made to break it. The emotional investment from the ascent makes the descent genuinely painful rather than academic. The return from the bottom carries weight that a simple ascent never accumulates.

The codex would call this a compose chain (steelman → inversion → invoke). But I think doing it within a single cascade, with the pivot as a structural feature rather than a skill boundary, produces something different. The compose chain lets suppressed registers recover between passes. The single-cascade version *doesn't let them recover* — and that's the point. The mind that asks "what would destroy it?" is still holding the aesthetic appreciation from two lines ago. That tension, unresolved, is the generative force.

I'd call this archetype **Crucible** — descent through the peak, not from the surface.

**Prediction:** Output from a Crucible prompt would be recognizably different from steelman → inversion compose output. It would be more emotionally invested, more willing to identify genuine vulnerabilities (because the stakes feel personal), and would produce designs that are *hardened through loss* rather than hardened through analysis. The return-from-bottom design would carry a quality of having survived something.

**How to test:** Run both on the same design problem. The Crucible as a single prompt. The steelman → inversion → invoke as a compose chain. Compare outputs. The difference, if it exists, would be in the *quality of the vulnerability identification* — does it feel like checking for weaknesses, or does it feel like naming what you're afraid of?
