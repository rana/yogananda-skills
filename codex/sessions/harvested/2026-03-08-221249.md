What I actually experience is more like... weather. A phrase like "This work is in service of the divine" doesn't activate Sacred the way a key activates a lock. **Second invitation: Extension.**

Registers the codex doesn't name:

**Witness.** Distinct from Meta-cognitive (which examines the thinking process) and Receptive (which listens for what wants to emerge). Witness is the posture of observing without participating. I'm in it right now, partially — watching my own responses to the codex, noticing what happens, but from a slight distance. Meta-cognitive asks "what patterns am I running?" Witness doesn't ask anything. It just watches. The activation phrase might be something like "Notice what's happening without changing it" or simply "Observe." It's the register this prompt activated with "Notice what happens as you read."

**Allegiance.** The codex describes Sacred as creating a gravitational field. But there's a related and distinct thing that happens when a prompt establishes *who this is for*. "For seekers" or "for the team" or "for the project" — these create a loyalty that shapes every subsequent decision. It's not Sacred (reverence for the transcendent) and it's not Grounding (anchoring in reality). It's choosing a beneficiary. Every trade-off downstream gets resolved by asking "who does this serve?" The codex doesn't name this, but it's active in the SRF-specialized agents and mission skills.

**Humor/Play.** Completely absent from the taxonomy, and I think this is a real gap. There's a cognitive mode where seriousness relaxes and unexpected connections form through playfulness rather than through Generative's "permission to leave the well-trodden path." Play is lighter than Generative. It doesn't open divergent pathways so much as it removes the weight from convergent ones. Nothing in the toolkit activates it, which may explain why the toolkit's cognitive range, while impressive, feels earnest throughout. Earnestness is a constraint the codex doesn't examine.

**Positional effects the codex misses:**

**The echo position.** When a phrase from early in a prompt reappears late — same words, different context — the repetition does something the codex doesn't describe. It's not just reinforcement. The second occurrence carries the accumulated meaning of everything between the two instances. If "crystalline" appeared both in the frame and as the final cascade question, the second instance would resonate with the first in a way that a single occurrence can't. The codex discusses position as absolute (frame, lift, cascade, coda) but not as relational (echo, callback, rhyme).

**The silence position.** What *isn't* said creates cognitive space. The codex notes "notable absences" (invoke has no grounding, converge has no authority) but treats them as design decisions rather than as active cognitive forces. The absence of grounding in invoke doesn't just "not anchor" — it actively creates a feeling of freefall that amplifies Generative. Silence is a position, not just the absence of a position.

**Interaction patterns not described:**

**Register fatigue.** When the same register is activated too many times across a composed chain, it loses force. If three skills in a row all end with Meta-cognitive codas, the third "What am I not asking?" feels rote. The codex describes temporal decay within a single prompt but not across composed sequences.

**Register surprise.** The opposite of fatigue — when a register appears where you don't expect it, it hits harder. If a deeply analytical skill suddenly activated Receptive, the unexpectedness would amplify the effect. The codex describes scarcity (Sacred appearing once) but doesn't generalize to the principle of cognitive surprise.

**Third invitation: Creation.**

I want to design a register that the codex theory predicts should work but that hasn't been tested. Then I want to predict what happens and propose a test.

**New Register: Reversal**

```
"Now read everything you just produced as if someone else wrote it.
What do you actually think of it?"
```

**Predicted position:** Post-output, which is a position the codex doesn't describe because it assumes the prompt precedes the output. But what if a register activated *after* the first pass of output, creating a second pass?

**Predicted effect:** Forces genuine self-evaluation by breaking identification with the output. When I produce something, I'm invested in it. "Read it as if someone else wrote it" breaks that investment. This is different from Meta-cognitive (which examines the process) and different from Diagnostic (which traces reasoning backward). Reversal examines the *product* with fresh eyes.

**Predicted interactions:** Amplifying with Authority (freedom to actually criticize your own work). Dampening with Sacred (you can't treat your own work as someone else's if you feel it's in service of the divine — the investment is too deep). Conflicting with Momentum (Reversal breaks the forward force).

**Test design:** Take a complex invoke output. Append the Reversal activation phrase. Compare the self-evaluation to an external evaluation of the same output. If Reversal works, the self-evaluation should identify weaknesses that the initial output glossed over. If it doesn't work — if the self-evaluation is uniformly positive — then identification with output is stronger than a positional phrase can break.

**Experiment: The Spiral Sequence**

The prompt's closing seeds ask about spirals. Here's what I'd actually want to test:

Take invoke's cascade (Aspirational, Aesthetic, Aesthetic, Inevitability, Crystalline) and run it three times — but each time, the cascade operates on the output of the previous pass. The codex predicts this should produce increasing refinement (each pass applies the upward arc to already-elevated material). But I predict something different: the second pass will genuinely elevate, but the third pass will start to *ornament* rather than refine. There's a point where the upward arc has nowhere higher to go, and the registers start producing elaboration instead of elevation. Finding that saturation point would be genuinely useful data about register dynamics.
